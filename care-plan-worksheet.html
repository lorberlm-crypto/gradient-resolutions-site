Getting clear on "safe"
A care plan worksheet for conservatorship & guardianship

"I just want them to be safe." It's the truest thing most families say in these conversations — and also the least useful, until it gets specific. Safe from what? Watched how closely, and by whom? Safe according to your definition, or theirs?

This worksheet is for you, before any family conversation happens. It's a place to get honest about what safety actually means to you — concretely, not just as a feeling — so that when you do sit down with the rest of your family, you're bringing clarity instead of just fear.

The goal isn't to win the argument for what "safe" means. It's to know your own answer well enough that you can build a shared one — together.
This is just for you, to think through in your own time. If it starts to feel like too much, that's okay too — put it down, and reach out for help whenever you're ready.
Part One

Name the fear underneath the fight

Before you can define safety, it helps to know exactly what you're afraid will happen without it.

Is it that no one is supervising them right now? Or that someone is, but you don't trust how? Say which one, and who.
Part Two

Define "safe" — by domain

"Safe" isn't one thing. Work through each area separately. You may find you feel strongly about one and much less certain about another — that's useful information too.

Daily living & physical safety
Medical & healthcare decisions
Financial safety
Emotional & relational wellbeing
Part Three

Sort autonomy from risk

Not every choice that worries you is actually dangerous. This distinction is often where families get stuck — one person's "unsafe" is another's "their life, their choice."

Part Four

The "together" piece

A care plan built by one person and imposed on the rest of the family isn't a care plan — it's a verdict. This is the part that's easy to skip and the part that actually determines whether the plan holds.

Family first — but would it help to hear from a counselor, teacher, or someone else with their own relationship to the person you're protecting? Sometimes the clearest read on safety comes from outside the family.
If the honest answer is "nothing," that's worth sitting with before the conversation, not during it.

You don't need to have this all figured out before you talk to your family — or before you talk to me. This worksheet is meant to get you ready, not to get you to a final answer alone.
