Writing your personal letter
A step-by-step guide for reaching out when the door has been closed

Your words. Not your attorney's. Not your mediator's.

A mediator can explain a process. A coach can explain a framework. But neither of them can do what only you can do: reach out as a person, not as a party in a dispute.

This letter is not a legal document. It is not a petition. It is a human gesture — one that says: I am willing to be humble before I am right.

Before you begin
Leave out your defenses entirely. This is the hardest instruction and the most important one. The moment your letter explains why you acted the way you did, or corrects their version of events, or reminds them of your good intentions — it stops being an invitation and starts being a brief. They have heard your defense before. They are not waiting for a better version of it. They are waiting to see if you can sit with the uncertainty of not knowing — and still reach out anyway.

Step 3

C.H.I.L.D. check

C.H.I.L.D. is Laura's own framework for communication in high-conflict family situations: Child-Focused, Honest, Intentional, Low-Heat, Direct. Run your letter through each test before you send it.
C Child-Focused
H Honest
I Intentional
L Low-Heat
D Direct
A note on this framework
C.H.I.L.D. is a communication method Laura developed for families in high-conflict situations. The goal: keep the people you love out of adult conflict, de-escalate rather than trigger defensiveness, and get your actual needs met instead of winning the argument. Writing this way isn't soft or passive — it's strategic.
