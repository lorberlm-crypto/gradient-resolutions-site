When a door is closed:
What mediation can offer families navigating estrangement
A resource for parents, grandparents, and anyone caught in the middle. Estrangement is one of the most painful experiences a family can face. When a grandparent is cut off from a grandchild, everyone loses something — and no one quite knows how to find their way back. This handout explains how mediation can help families explore what, if anything, is possible.

Why families stay stuck

Protecting your child is the right instinct. When there has been hurt, conflict, or a breakdown in trust, the instinct to close the door makes complete sense. Protecting your child — especially from something you can't fully predict or control — is not overreaction. It is good parenting.

But closed doors can become permanent walls. And permanent walls have a cost too: for the child, for the family, and sometimes for you. Many families stay stuck not because resolution is impossible, but because no one has created a safe enough space to even look at the question.

When it comes to keeping our children safe, we may be afraid to open the door even a crack. That fear is valid. A skilled mediator's job is to make opening that crack feel safe enough to try — without committing to anything more than a conversation.

What mediation is — and is not

Mediation isMediation is not
A confidential, voluntary processA legal proceeding or court order
A chance to speak and be heardA way to force anyone to do anything
Separate conversations first — you never have to be in the same room until you choose to beTaking sides or assigning blame
Focused on what you need, not on who was rightTherapy or counseling
Guided by a neutral third party with no stake in the outcomeA commitment to an ongoing relationship
Flexible — outcomes are shaped by you, not imposedSomething that requires you to forgive, reconcile, or agree

How it works in practice

We start separately — and only go further if it makes sense. In estrangement cases, the mediator meets with each party privately first. These are called individual sessions. They are a place to share your perspective, raise your concerns, and — most importantly — understand what you would need to feel safe before any joint conversation could happen.

No one is pushed toward a joint session before they are ready. And if the individual sessions reveal that a joint conversation is not possible or not helpful right now, that is a valid outcome too. You have learned something important, and you have done it safely.

What an agreement might look like

Every family situation is different. Some agreements are simple; some are detailed. The mediator helps you explore what would actually work — not what sounds good in theory.

Rules of engagement

Clear boundaries and expectations — what is okay, what is not, and who decides

Steps to rebuild trust

A structured path: actions one party takes over time before contact expands

A visitation agreement

Specific written terms for when, where, how often, and under what conditions contact occurs

A pause agreement

An agreed period of no contact, with defined conditions under which the conversation can reopen

A communication plan

How parties will handle future disagreements — so one conflict doesn't become another estrangement

No agreement — and that's okay

Sometimes the most important outcome is clarity about where each person stands, without pretending otherwise

You don't have to decide anything today. You don't have to agree to meet with anyone. You don't have to know what you want the outcome to be.

You only have to decide if you're willing to find out what's possible.
